Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a supercomputer to participate as a Solver?

No. While more powerful hardware will earn more, the network is designed to be open to a wide range of devices, including consumer grade GPUs found in many gaming PCs.

How does the network prove work is done correctly?

Gensyn uses advanced cryptographic techniques, including probabilistic checks and zk SNARKs. This allows Verifiers to confirm with very high certainty that a calculation is correct without needing to do the heavy computation themselves.

What is the Gensyn token used for?

The token is the native currency of the network. It is used by Submitters to pay for compute, by Solvers and Verifiers as a security deposit (stake) to ensure honest behavior, and ultimately to reward participants for their contributions (testnet).

What is Gensyn?

Here are a few ways to understand the project, from a simple overview to a deeper dive.

Explanation 1: The Simple Overview

Gensyn is a network that connects spare computer power from CPUs and GPUs around the world to train AI models. Users can share their unused device resources to complete AI tasks and receive payment.

In simple terms: Gensyn links devices into a large shared system. It operates without a central authority, using blockchain rules to manage tasks and payments securely. Users can submit AI training jobs, such as teaching an AI to identify objects, and others can process those jobs on their devices.

The main aims are to reduce AI training costs, provide open access, and ensure accuracy through verification methods. Its testnet, a free testing version, is active, with 149,000 users who have trained over 500,000 AI models. It works with various hardware: Basic CPUs on phones or laptops can handle small tasks, while GPUs support larger ones.

Explanation 2: The Two Brains of Your Computer

To understand why Gensyn is needed, you have to understand why AI training is so difficult. Imagine you want to teach a computer to recognize a cat. You must show it millions of pictures until it learns the pattern. This process takes an incredible amount of a specific kind of computer power.

The CPU: The General Manager

The CPU is the main brain, like a smart office manager who is great at handling thousands of different tasks one by one. It is a jack of all trades, but it can only focus on a few complex things at once.

The GPU: The Army of Specialists

The GPU was designed for video games. It is a master of one thing: doing thousands of simple, repetitive calculations all at the same time. Think of it like an army of 10,000 workers. AI training is a perfect job for this army.

The Big Problem: Computer Power is Expensive and Controlled

The most powerful GPUs are incredibly expensive and rare. Today, only a handful of giant technology companies own them in massive data centers. If a small company, researcher, or student wants to train a powerful AI, they have to rent time from these big companies. It is extremely expensive and centralized. This is the problem Gensyn solves.

How Gensyn Works

Explanation 1: The Group Project Analogy

Imagine you have a very big, difficult homework assignment, like building a giant model of a city. This is the "AI Job." Doing it all by yourself would take a very long time. Gensyn is like a group project system that lets you get help from thousands of classmates all over the world. Here's how it works in four simple steps:

1

You Post the Homework

You tell the Gensyn system, "I need help building this model city."

2

Gensyn Splits the Homework into Tiny Jobs

The system automatically breaks your big assignment into thousands of small, easy pieces. One piece is "build one small house," another is "build one tree." These small jobs are sent out to all the "helpers" on the network, which are just regular computers.

3

Helpers Do the Work and Show Proof

A helper computer does its small job. When finished, it shows a special proof, like a checklist showing every step it took. Other computers quickly look at this proof to confirm the work is correct without having to rebuild the house themselves.

4

Helpers Get Paid

As soon as the proof is checked and confirmed, the helper computer automatically gets a small payment. This repeats for every piece until the entire city is built, much faster and cheaper than doing it alone.

Explanation 2: The Method Explained

Gensyn is a worldwide computer network built for training Artificial Intelligence. It operates on its own blockchain to connect people who need computer power with those who have it to spare. The network involves three main groups of people:

Submitters

The customers who pay to have their AI models trained.

Solvers

The workers who connect their GPUs to perform training tasks for payment.

Verifiers

The quality checkers who confirm the Solvers' work is correct.

Gensyn has a unique four step process to make sure everything works securely and without trust:

  1. A Submitter sends their AI training job to the network. Solvers see this job and decide if they want to work on it.
  2. A Solver starts the training on their own computer. The network uses random check ins, asking the Solver to create a mathematical proof of its work so far. A Solver can't cheat, as they never know when a check in will happen.
  3. The proof is sent to the blockchain, where a team of Verifiers is randomly selected to check it. This checking process is designed to be very fast and easy.
  4. Once the Verifiers agree the proof is correct, the system automatically sends payment from the Submitter to the Solver. If the work is wrong, the Solver is punished, which discourages cheating.

Why Does Gensyn Matter?

Explanation 1: The Big Picture

Gensyn matters because it solves big problems in AI today and opens doors for a better future. AI is changing everything from medicine to entertainment, but right now, only a few large companies can build the best models. This creates unfair gaps. Gensyn fixes that by sharing compute power worldwide, making AI training affordable, open, and reliable for everyone. Here is why this is important, explained step by step with simple examples.

First, AI training is too expensive and limited.

Building a strong AI model needs massive computer power, like running thousands of GPUs for days. This costs thousands of dollars per job on cloud services. Small teams, schools, or inventors in developing countries cannot afford it. As a result, AI advances stay with giants like Google or OpenAI. Gensyn cuts costs by up to 80 percent. It does this by using spare hardware from everyday devices. A student wanting to train an AI for a farm app no longer needs a huge budget; they can post the job on Gensyn, and global solvers complete it cheaply.

Second, it wastes less energy and resources.

Computers around the world run at only about 40 percent capacity most of the time. Idle GPUs in homes and offices sit unused while new ones get made, which harms the environment. Gensyn connects this hidden power into one global network, scaling AI capacity without building extra hardware. Instead of a company buying 100 new GPUs, they tap into 1,000 idle ones from users worldwide, saving money and reducing e waste.

Third, it builds trust without middlemen.

In traditional AI, you must trust a cloud provider to run tasks correctly. Gensyn uses math proofs and blockchain to verify work automatically. The system checks results with just a fraction of the effort, catching errors or cheats. Payments happen instantly via smart contracts. A doctor training an AI on patient scans knows the work is accurate and private, thanks to encryption that hides data during training.

Fourth, it speeds up innovation for all.

AI demand doubles every three months. Without shared access, progress slows, and benefits go to the wealthy. Gensyn democratizes this, letting anyone contribute or use compute. Over 149,000 users have trained 500,000 models on its testnet already. Artists in remote areas can train custom image generators, or researchers in Africa can build local language translators, fostering diverse ideas.

Explanation 2: The Monopoly Problem in Detail

The Problem

Training powerful AI models requires an insane amount of specialized computer hardware (GPUs). Currently, only a handful of giant tech corporations can afford to build and run the massive data centers needed. This creates a dangerous bottleneck.

  • This centralizes control over the future of AI in the hands of a few.
  • It creates a huge financial barrier for startups and researchers.
  • Innovation is slowed down because only the biggest players can afford to experiment.

The Solution

Gensyn breaks this monopoly by creating a global, open marketplace for compute power. It is like a stock market for computer hardware, where supply and demand set a fair price.

  • Anyone can contribute their unused computer power and get paid.
  • AI training becomes affordable and accessible for everyone.
  • It dramatically speeds up innovation by letting more people build.
